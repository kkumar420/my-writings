# Na De Dil Pardesi Nu: Love, Self and the Dissolution of the Ego
### A Punjabi folk song — revived in Dhurandhar — about love that demands the end of the self

---

This Punjabi song recently gained attention when it was remixed for the title track of Dhurandhar. You can listen to the original version by Mohammed Sadiq and Ranjit Kaur [here](https://youtu.be/wfsYjGjjQOE?si=d94TxU5xg5rBqavi).

_Na de dil pardesi nu_  
  
_Tainu nit da rona pai jau ga_  
  
_Nal ranjhe tay jogi de tainu jogan hauna pai jau ga_

_Don’t give your heart to the stranger_  
  
_You will be made to weep every day_  
  
_With Ranjha, the Jogi — you will have to become a Jogan_

Most people who listen to this song would think it is about a girl hopelessly in love with a man. It is not.  
  
Beneath its surface, this song carries one of the most common motifs in mystical literature — **the Lover and the Beloved**.

## The Lover and the Beloved

In this song, the _Pardesi_ — the stranger, someone from a foregin land — is not a man whom the girl desires. He is Truth, Reality or God. _Pardesi_ because Truth does not belong to the world of conditioning, roles, social performance and ego-maintenance that we inhabit. He is foreign to the self we have constructed.

The girl is the human soul. Searching, longing and perpetually separated from what she most deeply is.

The framework of the Lover and the Beloved appears everywhere across traditions — in the works of Bhakti saints and Sufi poets, in Gurbani, even loosely in the fall of Adam and Eve from the Garden of Eden.

The Beloved wears different faces: Krishna, the Rose, the Akaal Purakh, the girl behind the closed door. The Lover appears as the Nightingale, the soul-bride, the boy who knocks and is turned away. But the structure is always the same — an all-consuming longing, a cost that looks like destruction from the outside, and a union that is only possible through the **dissolution of the one who was seeking**.

## The Poison

_Mai ishq de alleya zakhman de khud has has ke muh si langi_  
  
_Je yaar mera mainu zehar deve, mai ghat ghat kar ke pee langi_

_The wounds of love — I will cover them myself, smiling_  
  
_If my beloved offers me poison, I will drink it, sip by sip_

What separates the girl from her Beloved is not distance. It is the structure she carries inside her — her conditioning, her past impressions, her sense of being a separate self, with things to protect and roles to maintain. **This structure itself is the separation**. And the poison the Beloved offers is precisely the dissolution of this structure.

To the ego, this feels like death. Because it is — the ego’s death. The removal of conditioning is not a gentle process. It is destabilizing and frightening because everything you have taken yourself to be begins to loosen. The ego actively resists and when cornered, produces suffering. This is why the poison tastes like poison.

But the girl drinks it anyway because her love for the Beloved is greater than her attachment to the self that would be lost.

**Kabir** says:

_प्रेम न बाड़ी उपजे, प्रेम न हाट बिकाए_  
  
_राजा प्रजा जो ही रुचे, सिस दे ही ले जाए_

_Love does not grow in gardens, nor is it sold in markets_  
  
_King or commoner, whoever desires it — must offer their head and take it_

The head — not the heart, not effort, not good intentions. The head, which is the symbolic seat of the ego, the centre of the constructed self. This is the price of entry and it is non-negotiable.

On the day of the creation of the Khalsa, Guru Gobind Singh Ji gathered thousands of his Sikhs, drew his sword, and asked for a volunteer to offer their head. The crowd fell silent. Out of thousands, only five people stood up — one by one, walking into the tent from which none appeared to return. These five — the Panj Pyaare or the Beloved Five — embody the essence of Kabir’s verse. The Guru was not asking for devotion, or faith, or years of service but the head. And when those five offered it without condition, he gave back not death but the Amrit of immortality and initiation into a new life.

Meera too, uncompromising in her love and devotion to Krishna, drank the poison which her husband Rana had sent with a smile. It is said in popular tradition that the poison had no effect on her due to the divine intervention of Krishna.

There is also this story by Rumi, of a boy who knocks on the door of his beloved. She asks: _who is there?_ He says: _it is I._ The door stays shut. He goes away, returns transformed, and knocks again. She asks again: _who is there?_ This time he says: _it is thou._ The door opens.

The poison’s function is to kill the _I_ that was knocking. Not to punish the boy, but to make union structurally possible. Two cannot fit in the lane of love and so one of them must disappear. Either the “I” or the Truth.

**Kabir:**  
  
_जब मैं था तब गुरु नहीं, अब गुरु हैं मैं नाहिं_  
  
_प्रेम गली अति सांकरी, तामें दो न समाहिं_

_When I was, the Beloved was not. Now the Beloved is, and I am not._  
  
_The lane of love is very narrow — two cannot fit within it._

## Becoming the Jogan

_Nal ranjhe tay jogi de tainu jogan hauna pai jau ga_

_With Ranjha, with the Jogi — you will have to become a Jogan_

Society delivers this as a warning. The girl receives it as a statement of fact she has already accepted.

A _Jogi_ or a _Jogan_ is a renunciant — not the forest-dwelling ascetic but one who has given up the social roles, the respectability, the pre-built architecture of a life of external approval. Becoming a Jogan means the collapse of everything the ego has organized itself around. And the ego, naturally, presents this as loss — of potential, of future, of the life that was promised.

Guru Nanak Ji returns to this image repeatedly. The human soul is a bride, separated from her _Khasam_ — the bridegroom, the Akaal Purakh, the Timeless One. The _viyog_, the separation, is not merely sorrow but a furnace. The longing itself is what prepares the bride for union. A bride who feels no separation has not yet begun to love. And a soul that has not yet begun to ache for the Beloved is still fast asleep inside its own conditioning.

The Sufis tell the story of the Gul and the Bulbul — the Rose and the Nightingale. The Nightingale is mad in love for the Rose and sings all night the most beautiful songs in longing. But the Rose is white, cold and unresponsive — the Nightingale knows that to truly reach her, singing alone is not enough. It flies to the Rose and presses its breast against the sharpest thorn on the stem. The thorn enters the heart. The Nightingale does not pull away. It presses deeper, still singing, pouring its life into the wound — and as its blood runs down the stem and into the petals, the Rose slowly turns red. The Nightingale dies. The Rose blooms in a color the world had never seen before. And ever since, roses have been red.

## Stripping the Metaphor

This metaphor-ridden language risks being dismissed as esoteric, vague-sounding mysticism. But the psychological meaning behind the metaphors has to be understood.

The Beloved is not a figure in the sky demanding devotion. The mystics needed a face — Krishna, the Rose, the Bridegroom — because the ego finds it difficult to surrender to an abstraction. It needs something to move toward. But what the Lover is ultimately moving toward is not an external destination but the falling away of the structure that prevented them from seeing clearly in the first place.

When Nanak’s soul-bride longs for her Khasam, she is describing — in the language of love and longing — the same movement that the psychological inquiry of J. Krishnamurti points at: the recognition that what we call “I” is a construction, maintained by memory, conditioning, and the continuous need to protect itself. The Beloved is the name the poets gave to what remains when that construction is no longer in the way.

## What We Call Love

Our conception of love is very different from the love of Kabir, Meera, Nanak or Rumi.

It is ego-consolidation — desire (primarily sexual), possession, validation, _has to be mine_. The moment the object of love stops serving the ego’s need, love ends. This is the ego using the word love to describe its own hunger.  
  
(a short one-page story if it interests you - [The Sleepwalkers by Khalil Gibran](https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poems/58695/the-sleep-walkers))

The love this song describes is entirely different — one of total surrender and dissolution. Meera danced in devotion to Krishna, uncaring of her social position or her modesty as defined by the conventions of the time. She had already offered the head and there was nothing left to protect or fear. The Nightingale pressed its heart onto the thorn not because it was promised something in return, but because the love had grown larger than the self.

**Kabir:**  
  
_प्रेम पियाला सो पिये, शीश दक्षिणा देय_  
  
_लोभी शीश न दे सके, नाम प्रेम का लेय_

_Only one who offers their head can drink from love’s cup_  
  
_The covetous cannot offer the head — yet they take the name of love_

## A Life Wasted

_Je pyar nahi, dildar nahi, kihde kam husn jawani ae?_  
  
_Eh ishq bina bas mehkan vihuni, phull vargi zindagani ae_  
  
_Main bhar joban vich eh ho jehi, zindagi jee ke ki laangi?_

_If there is no love, no beloved — what use is beauty, what use is youth?_  
  
_A life without this love is like a flower without fragrance_  
  
_At the peak of my youth — what is the point of living like this?_

This is where the song stops being poetry and becomes an indictment.

You are right that this is the peak of your life. The girl agrees. But her conclusion is the opposite of yours. You think the peak of life is the time to secure, to achieve, to build the structures that will carry you safely through. She says: if not now, when? You will spend this time managing your career, your parents, your children, your image — and when it is over, you will have tended everything except the one thing that mattered.

**Kabir:**  
  
_चलती चक्की देख के, दिया कबीरा रोए।  
  
दो पाटन के बीच में, साबुत बचा न कोए।।_

_Watching the grinding mill, Kabir wept_  
  
_Between the two stones, nothing comes out whole_

To the society, the girl is wasting her life. To herself, anything less would be a wasted life.
