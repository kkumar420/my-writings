# The Unexamined Subject
### An inquiry into the nature of the “I” and the origin of psychological suffering

## 1. The Aim

Who are you?

Beyond your name, history, or roles — what is it that actually constitutes this “I” that is referred to so casually?

This is not a religious question, nor a metaphysical indulgence. It is a direct psychological inquiry, the most fundamental of all questions that can be asked and unfortunately one that is not touched upon either due to ignorance, or worse, purposefully ignored due to the subconscious fear of what one might find inside of themselves. The aim here is not to construct a new belief or idea, nor to arrive at some comforting conclusion, but to see clearly and uncover what is false.

---

## 2. The Error in Approach

Life, as it is lived by most of us, is full of suffering and conflict which manifests as anxiety about the future, regret about the past, dissatisfaction in the present at the personal level and wars, poverty, environmental degradation at the global level.

Our instinct, right from birth, is to look outward. A child does not have much capacity for introspection, and by the time she develops the mental maturity to exercise this faculty, she has already absorbed a dense web of conditioning from her surroundings — habitual ways of thinking, inherited notions of right and wrong, ideas of success and failure, patterns of approval, belonging and fear. Her attention is almost always directed outward and is rarely turned inward to examine her own experience. Instead, this web of conditioning is continuously reinforced over time. Unless interrupted, this pattern persists across a lifetime and this conditioning is transmitted to the next generation. In this way, cycles of unexamined living are perpetuated.

The fact of suffering is undeniable. But in saying that “there is suffering”, attention remains fixed on the object of distress, while the subject — the one who perceives this suffering — remains unexamined. Who exactly is the one who suffers?

To investigate suffering without first examining the structure of the sufferer is a fundamental error in priority. We attempt to manage external circumstances or suppress internal impressions, yet we resist examining the “I” that experiences them. It is this mistake that allows suffering to persist.

---

## 3. The Constructed Self

What we call the “I” or the ego, is not a fixed entity.

At the most basic level, what is observable is an organism — a complex biological system responding to its environment. Like other animals, it is capable of pain, fear, and distress. These are direct responses based on sense data and therefore are immediate and situational.

What distinguishes us is not the presence of pain or pleasure, but the emergence of a psychological structure capable of organizing experience into a narrative. The brain does not merely register events passively, but actively interprets, remembers and projects. Out of this movement, a centre is constructed, an apparent “owner” of experience.

This is what we call the “I” or the ego.

It is not a material object that can be located, nor an essence within, but a **functional abstraction** — a continuously updated construct formed through memory, thought, and identification. It binds separate experiences into a coherent story: this happened to me, this defines me, this should not have happened, this must happen again.

Biological pain (injury or illness) is a physical fact. But psychological suffering different. It is the kind of distress that lingers, repeats, and intensifies long after the event has passed. It is generated when the constructed “I” interprets incoming experience through conditioning and past impressions — traces which have no current reality.

In this sense, the ego is not merely a victim of suffering. It is the mechanism through which suffering, as we ordinarily know it, is sustained and amplified.

---

## 4. The Mechanism of the Ego

To understand the ego, one must see how it operates — not as theory, but in direct experience.

### 4.1 Identification

The ego begins with identification.

The most fundamental form of identification is that with the body. An infant does not have a clear sense that the body is “mine” from birth. She touches and explores her hands and feet as if they are part of her external environment.

Over time, she begins to notice a pattern: when she intends to move, this body moves. When this body is touched, sensations of touch, pleasure, or pain arise. But when objects other than her body interact among themselves, no such sensations are registered.  
  
Through this repeated correlation, a distinction is gradually formed. This body is treated as a distinct centre of experience, set apart from the rest of the environment.

Of course at a functional level this distinction is necessary for coordination and survival. The organism must differentiate between what it can regulate and what it cannot.

But from this a further step is taken, which is no longer merely functional.  
  
The body is not only used as a centre of action, but a crystallized static “me” is assumed which supposedly owns the experience.

This can be seen directly.  
  
A sensation arises — a sharp pain, for instance, from some external stimulus. In the immediate moment it is simply a sensation. But almost instantly, thought intervenes: _“I am in pain”._ The sensation is no longer merely present; it is owned.

Or consider movement. An intention arises, and the hand lifts. The mind immediately frames it as: _“I moved my hand”_. Yet in direct observation, there is only an intention followed by a movement. The sense of a central “I” which initiates and owns this process is not directly encountered, it is inferred.

Note that it may appear that intention requires an “I” to which it has to belong. But this does not hold under observation. Intention arises in the same way as sensation, part of the same process. It is only afterward that it is claimed: _“I intended”, “I decided”._

Where, then, does this “I” enter? It does not appear as a participant in the process, but as an interpretation layered upon it.

Thus, identification with the body is not false in the sense that the body is “unreal”, but that the ownership attributed to its functions is a mental addition.

Here, a natural objection arises: is not this interpretive movement itself part of the same process of nature, since the ego too is a product of the human brain — shaped through evolution?

It is. The formation of a stable sense of “I” arises naturally through memory and repeated patterns of thought. But the question is not whether the ego exists as a natural phenomenon, but whether what it claims to be is true.

Naturalness does not guarantee that an impression is a fact. A mirage is a natural optical occurrence, yet the water it seems to suggest is an illusion. Similarly, the arising of the ego-pattern is a biological fact, but the conclusion that this pattern is an independent, permanent “subject” is an error.

What is observed are thoughts, sensations and reactions arising and passing. The sense of a separate entity that owns or controls them is not encountered, but is merely assumed.

This assumption introduces a division. Experience is split into an observer and something being observed. But in direct observation, what is found is a single movement — thoughts, sensations, and reactions arising together, not two separate entities.  
  
From this division arises conflict — resistance to what is felt, attachment to what is desired, and the constant attempt to control or modify experience.

From this primary identification with the body, all further identifications follow.

The body is taken to be “me”. Thoughts are taken to be “my thoughts”. Emotions become “my feelings”. Roles — student, friend, son — are absorbed into identity.

What is essentially a fluid set of processes is gathered under a single label: “I”.

---

### 4.2 Conditioning and Psychological Time

Beneath identification lies conditioning and conditioning is inseparable from psychological time.

The mind is shaped by accumulated memory — past experiences, cultural input, repeated patterns of thought and emotion. These leave behind impressions, which in turn influence how new experiences are perceived.

What is seen is not merely the present, but the present as interpreted through these accumulated impressions.

This creates a self-reinforcing movement: past impressions shape present perception and present perception forms new impressions that are added to memory. The past is continuously carried forward — not as something actually present, but as memory influencing interpretation of experience.

This movement is psychological time.

The past appears as memory: “what happened to me”.  
  
The future appears as projection: “what will happen to me”.  
  
Between the two, the present is rarely encountered directly.

Yet what is actually present is always immediate — this sensation, this perception, this moment of awareness. The future or the past have no reality in the current, present moment.

Thus, the “I” is not only constructed through identification, but sustained through psychological time as a continuity of what I have been, what I am and what I will or want to become.  
  
Without this continuity, maintained through memory and projection, the sense of a stable “I” cannot persist.

A further layer of thought needs to be noted of.  
  
When a thought arises — fear, desire, anger — it is almost immediately followed by judgment: _this is good_, _this is wrong_, _this should not be happening_, _this must continue_.  
  
But this judgment too is not independent and arises from the same conditioning. The thought itself is immediate but the judgment about it comes from memory.

Thus, the present movement of thought is not only shaped by the past, but also evaluated by the past.

If this is not seen, the mind remains caught in a cycle — reaction followed by judgment, judgment reinforcing reaction — without ever encountering the present directly.

**Note:**  
  
A word of caution here. If, after reading this, you attempt to distance yourself from a memory or suppress it, then that too is a reaction — arising from your memory of what is being read here.  
  
One cannot step outside this movement through effort.

---

### 4.3 Image Construction and Relationships

The same process extends to how we see others.  
  
We do not encounter people directly, but through images formed from past interactions, expectations, and memory.

A remark made yesterday, a remembered tone of voice, a moment of hurt or pleasure — these are carried forward in psychological time and shape how the person is seen in the present.

Thus, what is seen is not the person as they are in the present, but the image the mind has built around them.

Relationships then, are not between two people but between two sets of images, each responding to the image formed by the other.  
  
As long as these images interact, direct contact is lost and relationships remain confined within the past.

**Note**:  
  
To see another “freshly” does not mean to discard all past knowledge, but to be aware when that knowledge becomes an image that dictates reaction.

---

### 4.4 The Observer Problem

At this point, a critical question must be asked:  
  
**Who is it that observes the ego?**

At first glance, it appears that there is an observer — a “me” who is watching thoughts, emotions, and reactions.  
  
But if this is examined more closely, a complication becomes evident.

When a thought arises — fear, irritation, desire — it is often followed by another thought:  
  
_“I shouldn’t feel this”_,  
  
_“Why am I thinking this?”_  
  
_“This is wrong”_.

This, which appears to be observation, is in fact just another layer of reaction.  
  
The so called observer is simply another thought — arising from memory, shaped by conditioning, and responding according to past impressions. This is just thought reacting to thought and movement within the same structure. The ego has divided itself into an observer and the observed, but both belong to the same process.

But there is another possibility.  
  
A thought or emotion can be present without immediate judgment or interference. In such a case, there is awareness of what is happening, but no sense of a separate centre standing apart and observing it. Observation takes place, but there is no psychological observer.

Note that introducing the idea of a “higher self” or “pure consciousness” that performs this observation introduces a new identity — a subtler version of the same structure. The mind, having seen through one form of the ego, recreates it at a finer level. There is no need to posit a higher self. What is present is simply observation.

Now, one may object: _is such observation — without judgment or interference—actually possible?_  
  
Ordinarily, it does not seem so. Thought arises, and almost immediately, another thought comments on it—judging, comparing, correcting. This movement is so quick and habitual that it appears inseparable from the original perception itself.

But this does not mean that observation without judgment is impossible.  
  
Even the judgment — _“this is wrong”, “this should not be happening”_ — is itself a thought arising from your conditioning and that too has to be observed as such without suppressing or resisting it.  
  
“Just observation” is not something to be achieved. It is what remains when even the attempt to control or modify experience is seen as part of the same movement.

---

## 5. The Consequence

From this structure, psychological suffering arises.

The division between “me” and what is happening creates constant friction between what one wants and what is actually happening.

The mind moves between past and future:

- replaying what has happened with hurt, regret, memory
    
- anticipating what might happen with hope, anxiety, uncertainty
    

You are always fragmented between the traumas of your past and the dread of what lies in the future, and in this process, are never actually there in the present moment, which alone exists.

This same structure is operating at the collective level as well. Identification with religion, nation, ideology creates larger images:

- my belief
    
- my country
    
- my people
    

These identifications, often sensitive, are defended, compared, opposed. What begins as psychological division within the individual manifests itself as conflict between groups on the outside.

The point here is this: this is not just intellectual philosophizing. This is an immediate problem, in fact, the most urgent problem.

Physical pain, loss, and difficulty are part of life.  
  
But psychological suffering is sustained through the movement of thought and identification.  
  
Without the structure of the ego, pain may still arise, but it will not accumulate or become identity and continue as conflict with reality.

---

## 6. The Turning Point

If the ego is the source of suffering, **can it be eliminated?**

The immediate impulse is to try to control it, suppress it, reshape it, or replace it with something better.  
  
But as we saw before, any such effort is itself part of the same movement, the one who tries to change the ego is not separate from it—just another expression of it.

Thus, every attempt to eliminate the ego only strengthens it in a subtler form. It is not something that can be removed through effort.

What matters then is not removal, but seeing the movement of the ego as it unfolds.

---

## 7. Seeing

As seen, effort cannot resolve this, because any attempt to control or reshape the ego remains part of the same movement.

What matters is simply to see, because clear perception itself negates what is false.

When a thought is seen as a thought, it is no longer taken to be “me” When a reaction is seen as conditioned, its hold begins to loosen. When a narrative is seen as constructed, its authority begins to weaken.

Nothing is suppressed or consciously replaced. Conditioning continues as long as it operates unnoticed. False identification ends through being seen.

When a reaction is seen as it arises—without justification, without resistance—it does not reinforce itself in the same way.  
  
The cycle described earlier:

reaction → impression → reinforcement  
  
begins to loosen, because the reaction is no longer blindly identified with.

Thoughts, reactions, memories will continue to arise, but they are no longer identified as belonging to a centre. What arises is seen, and in being seen, its continuity is broken.

---

### Where Observation Stops

But this is not so easy. The ego is not a passive structure, it protects itself.  
  
Identification deepens into attachment, and the longer you identify with an idea, belief or a person, the more protective the ego becomes. Any external stimuli threatening what you hold dear is fiercely resisted.

This is why observation rarely goes very far. The moment something sensitive is touched, the mind resists — you feel hurt, insecure or emotionally disturbed.

But this resistance is actually a blessing in disguise, because it tells of our attachments which might not be as obvious otherwise. This hurt or insecurity needs to be observed without judgement or suppression.  
  
But this requires honesty and courage, to remain with what is seen, especially when it is uncomfortable. Otherwise, the mind moves away and the process stops.

One does not stop because one cannot see — but because seeing becomes uncomfortable.

---

## 8. Action as a Mirror

Until now, the emphasis has been on observing the structure of the ego directly. Which is not easy. The mind that attempts to observe is itself conditioned—driven by fear, desire, and habit. Observation, therefore, can easily become analysis, judgment, or another subtle form of self-improvement.

If the ego appears too subtle to observe directly, it can be seen in action. Action does not arise in a vacuum, it emerges from conditioning and therefore our inner structure is reflected in our actions. In this sense, we can observe action instead, which is a mirror to our ego.

When observed, it becomes clear that when the “I” acts, it acts toward its own continuity, seeking to secure, achieve, or become. In this movement, action is bound to psychological time. What is generally called “attachment to results” is not something added afterward, but is inherent in the structure from which action arises.  
  
This also clarifies a common misunderstanding. It is often assumed that one can act from ambition or self-interest and yet remain inwardly detached from the outcome. But if the action originates from a centre which seeks continuity, it is already bound to what it seeks.  
  
Because of this, attachment cannot be separated from action as a later adjustment. It is present in the movement itself.

When there is no demand for a psychological outcome, the quality of action changes. Attention is no longer divided between what is being done and what it might lead to.  
  
In this, the distinction between the doer and the doing begins to fade. Action still takes place, but without the same sense of a centre directing it towards a psychological result.  
  
The impressions of past and future lose some of their hold, notions like success and failure no longer carry the same psychological weight.

Note that this change does not come about as a result of applying a method, but by observation of how action is shaped by the need for continuity. As that movement is observed in action, its grip begins to loosen.  
  
In this way, action is a means to continuously observe the ego.

---

## 9. Living Without Conflict

As conditioning weakens, a different mode of living emerges. Action is no longer driven by self-centred projection or resistance. There is no longer a demand that reality conform to personal narratives. One moves with the flow of life, rather than against it. Note that this is not passiveness, but the absence of psychological conflict. When the mind is no longer burdened by its own constructions, action becomes clear, direct, and appropriate to the moment.

Such a person’s inner free state is reflected in the outer world by their great, fearless and bold actions, undertaken for the benefit of entire humankind. Only this deserves to be called real love.

This state of living is not just theoretical but examples of such lives are abundant throughout the world — Krishna, Kabir, Buddha, Rumi, Guru Nanak and Guru Gobind Singh, Meera, Jesus, Al Hallaj and countless others.

Apotheosis of these great people is just a trick of the ego, to create an unassailable gap between them and us. It provides an excuse to the ego for self-preservation, to dismiss the possibility that the heights achieved by these people are possible for us.

There is no such thing as divine or extraordinary. Everything has to conform to the laws of nature, and these people walked on this earth with the same body as us. Instead of blindly worshipping or copying them, their lives need to be remembered as testimony to the fact that freedom from psychological suffering is possible even in the most adverse conditions and such freedom is transformative. But it is achievable only if we choose to see ourselves with honesty, which requires immense courage.

## Sources

These insights are not novel. They are the synthesis of a common underlying mechanism of many philosophical and mystical traditions. Whether through the Aham of Vedanta, the Haumai of Sikhism, or the Nafs of Sufism, these traditions point to the same functional error: the ego as the primary engine of psychological suffering.

My aim was to strip away the layers of mysticism and metaphysics of these traditions and present their core psychological essence in modern language. Much has been borrowed from or influenced by Gita’s Krishna, Gautama Buddha, The Sikh Gurus — especially Guru Nanak, Guru Tegh Bahadur and Guru Gobind Singh — Tolstoy’s Jesus, Rumi, Attar of Neishapur, Kabir Bulleh Shah, Jiddu Krishnamurti, Acharya Prashant, Khalil Gibran and Osho.
