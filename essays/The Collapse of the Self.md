## 1. The Breaking Point

There come moments in life when the ground beneath one’s feet simply vanishes.

Not the small, familiar losses of life — disappointments, failures, the ordinary grief of things ending — but a catastrophic blow. Everything that one has loved, built, and become, gone in such rapid succession that the mind has no time to absorb one blow before the next arrives.

What happens to a person in that moment? What happens to the self-concept when every last thing it was built upon is stripped away?

To answer this, let us look at the lives of two people for whom this was not a theoretical question.

---

## 2. An Unbearable Reality

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Tk85!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6e86e2a9-7747-4338-948a-733638ccedaf_597x837.png)

_Leonardo DiCaprio as Andrew Laeddis in Shutter Island_

[_Shutter Island_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shutter_Island_\(film\)) is a strong contender to be my favourite movie. Not merely because of the plot or DiCaprio’s acting, but because I believe the movie carries a warning for all of us — there is very little that separates us from the protagonist, Andrew Laeddis.

Before the catastrophe, Andrew’s life was, by every conventional measure, a good one. He had served as a soldier in the Second World War, participating in the liberation of the Dachau concentration camp. After the war he became a US Marshal. He had a wife he loved and three children he adored, living the ideal householder’s life.

Later, however, his wife turns manic-depressive and burns down their apartment in a mental episode. Andrew, unable or maybe unwilling to face the reality of her illness, chose not to seek professional treatment and instead moved the family to a lakeside house — hoping perhaps that a change of environment would help her recover.

What happened next is the [most bone-chilling scene](https://youtu.be/-ZOifRi0-lY?si=L-8Xju0ErQX7x-KB) in the entire movie.

Andrew comes home from work one day to find his wife sitting alone by the lake. When he asks her where the kids are, she tells him they are at school, even though it is a Saturday. That is when he notices, to his horror, his three children floating face-down on the surface of the lake. His wife had drowned all three of them and later suggested that they keep their bodies as living dolls. After he brings the bodies back from the lake, she comes to him, asking to be set free — to be relieved of the torment inside her. Andrew, his three dead children lying on the bank beside him, shoots her after saying that he loves her.

In a matter of minutes, he loses everyone he dearly loved.

His mind was not able to accept what had happened. Not the grief alone, but the guilt compounding it — the knowledge that he had been warned, that the signs were right in front of him and he looked away, that his own evasion had cost his children their lives and ultimately his wife’s too, who he was left with no choice but to kill. The force of these colliding impressions — the sudden present horror violently clashing against years of accumulated love, memory, and identity — was simply too great for his mind to bear. Unable to bear reality, his mind invented another world for itself. He became “Teddy Daniels,” a US Marshal investigating a conspiracy on a remote island. He could not live in the world that contained the consequences of what had happened.

Note the precision of the delusion — it was not random madness. It preserved exactly what he wanted to preserve and discarded what caused him unbearable conflict. In Teddy Daniels’s world, he is still a US Marshal — his professional identity intact. His wife is recast as a victim of an arsonist named “Andrew Laeddis” rather than a woman he killed. His guilt is projected outward onto this phantom. The ego, even in its collapse, didn’t dissolve but rebuilt itself on a foundation of fiction, because it could not survive the foundation of fact anymore.

## 3. The Rupture

Andrew Laeddis is a fictional character. His tragedy might therefore be casually dismissed or held at a distance. So let us take a look at the life of [Bhikkhuni Patachara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patacara) — who was amongst the foremost disciples of the Buddha.

Patachara was the only daughter of wealthy and over-protective parents. She fell in love with a servant of the household, and knowing that her parents would not approve of the match, eloped with him and the two got married. Some years later, their first son was born.

When she was pregnant the second time, she asked her husband to take her back to her parents’ home, as was the custom during those times. Along the way, just as she was going into labour, a storm broke. Her husband asked her to wait by the side of the road while he entered the forest to cut some branches to make a shelter. Patachara waited for a long time, but he did not return. In the middle of the night, surrounded by rain and wind, she gave birth alone. At dawn she found her husband dead in the forest — killed by a poisonous snakebite.

She wept for a long time. Then she walked on, carrying her newborn in one arm and holding her older son’s hand with the other. She reached a river swollen from the storm. The water was too deep for the older child to cross unaided. She told him to wait on the bank while she carried the infant across first, holding him over her head against the current. When she was halfway across, a hawk swept down from the sky and seized the baby from her raised arms. She screamed at the bird, but it flew away with the child.

Her older son, standing on the far bank, heard her cries and thought she was calling him to cross. She saw him stepping into the rushing waters and shouted at him to wait. But it was too late — the strong currents swept him away, and she was unable to save him.

She stood alone on the riverbank, having lost her husband and both children, left with nothing.

She then walked for several days until she reached her parents’ home. When she finally arrived, she was told that her parents’ house had collapsed in the storm during the night, killing them both. She had arrived on the day of their cremation.

She collapsed by the road, no longer wanting to live.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QGeK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F974e0cd2-569f-4258-97d8-bf3af054cabc_1472x1069.png)

The rupture was complete. Patachara, like Andrew, lost everything that had constituted her world — husband, children, parents. The effect was not merely grief. The accumulated violence of these impressions shattered the coherent frame through which she had perceived herself and the world. She began to wander naked through the city, screaming, tearing off any clothing people tried to put on her, incoherent. She was abused, mocked, and driven from wherever she went — a woman everyone had decided was simply mad.

## 4. A House of Sand

Both Andrew Laeddis and Patachara broke under the same mechanism.

It was not merely that they suffered great losses. Human beings endure great losses. What shattered them was the total destruction of the structure upon which their sense of self had been built. Their identities were relational — constituted entirely through their roles and the people those roles depended upon. When those relationships were annihilated, there was no self left that knew how to exist. The question “who am I?” suddenly had no answer, and the mind, unable to tolerate that void, either invented a new answer (Andrew) or ceased to function coherently at all (Patachara).

The psychological mechanism here was described in my [first essay](https://open.substack.com/pub/kkumar420/p/the-unexamined-subject?): the ego is not a fixed entity but a construct, assembled from identification, memory and psychological time. It is the story we tell about who we are, and a story, however vivid, however long-running, is only as durable as the material it is made from. Andrew and Patachara had built their stories from material that could be taken away, and tragically, it was.

Of course, the probability that any one person will face a catastrophe of such magnitude is very low. And therefore, most people will read of Andrew and Patachara with pity and gratitude — _how tragic, but thank God that wasn’t me_. But this is precisely where the self-deception begins, because the disease is not the catastrophe. The catastrophe only _revealed_ the disease. The disease is the architecture of identity itself — the building of the self upon that which is impermanent.

You may not lose your children in a lake on a Saturday afternoon. But the people you love will die. The wealth you have accumulated will diminish. The body you identify with will weaken and betray you. The beliefs that have given your life coherence may one day stop being believable. Every element of the phenomenal world — everything that “comes into being” — will necessarily come to an end. That which has come, has to go.

**Psychological collapse occurs when identity is built upon things that can be taken away.**

To build the self upon such foundations — upon other people, upon roles, upon possessions, upon ambitions and hopes, upon even one’s own body and mind and intellect — is to live in a house made of sand, serene only because the tide has not yet come in.

We are all at present standing at the edge of a precipice — not so far from Andrew and Patachara as we would like to think. We simply don’t feel it, because the ground has not yet given way.

## 5. Last Man Standing

Now let us look at two lives that were tested just as severely — and did not break.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7Ycn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F177ac5c1-99f0-43f4-b631-4126a58a2190_906x1200.jpeg)


Everyone in India knows of [Krishna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krishna_in_the_Mahabharata). Most worship, praise and glorify him. We all have heard of his leelas with the gopis in Vrindavan, his slaying of Kamsa, and most importantly, his role in the Mahabharata, where he delivered to Arjuna and all mankind the timeless message of the Bhagavad Gita.

What is far less known and spoken of is what happened after the war.

Krishna’s life from birth was marked by relentless difficulties that would have broken most men before they reached adulthood. He was born under a death sentence from his own uncle Kamsa and had to be separated from his parents in the middle of the night. His childhood was filled with constant assassination attempts by assassins who have been portrayed in texts as supernatural Asuras. He left Vrindavan as a teenager to go to Mathura and kill Kamsa, never to return to his childhood home again. Kamsa’s father-in-law, Jarasandha, attacked Mathura repeatedly to avenge the killing. To protect his people, Krishna made the painful decision to relocate the entire Yadava clan from Mathura to the western coast of India, where he founded Dwarka — willing to set aside pride for the welfare of those in his care.

He spent his life preventing chaos, defeating tyrants, protecting the weak, and finally steering the [Mahabharata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahabharata) toward its terrible conclusion — ensuring the defeat of Duryodhana, though at the cost of an entire civilization.

After the end of the war, Gandhari, the mother of the Kauravas who were all killed in the war, held Krishna personally responsible for the war and the deaths of her sons. She cursed him that his own clan would destroy themselves in fratricidal violence, just as the Kurus had — and that he would witness it, helpless. Krishna, like everything else in his life before, accepted this curse too with a smile on his face.

Thirty-six years after the end of the Mahabharata, the [curse was fulfilled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mausala_Parva) exactly as she had said.

Krishna, right in front of his own eyes, had to watch the entire male population of his clan kill each other in a massive fratricidal massacre. His sons, brothers, uncles, cousins — everyone killed each other. Krishna too killed the Yadavas who came at him, and at the end, everyone except him was dead. He stood alone amongst the corpses of his entire clan.

Having lost everything, he walked back to Dwarka — now an empty city, a city of widows and old men — and went to his father Vasudeva. Just imagine that walk, through the streets of a capital whose men are all gone, to tell an aged father that the dynasty is finished. He told Vasudeva what had happened and asked him to send word to Arjuna to come and escort the surviving women to safety.

Then Krishna went out of the city, sat alone beneath a tree, and waited.

He knew perfectly well that no worldly power, no kingdom, no dynasty, no clan escapes time. And he had fought anyway, with everything he had, without demanding anything in return.

A hunter who mistook the resting Krishna for a deer struck him. And so the great Vasudeva Krishna died a solitary death.

Throughout all of this — the losses, the exile, the fratricidal horror, and the quiet end — no psychological collapse. Not even a tremor. In fact, in popular memory, Krishna is always portrayed with a slight, gentle smile, as if he knows something we do not.

## 6. The Flame and the Sparks

Many people will raise the objection that Krishna is a mythological figure whose historicity cannot be established. Whether Krishna was a historical person seems to me the least important question that could be asked about him. What matters is that the Gita exists to guide us.

But for those who require the testimony of documented history, here is a man whose life was surprisingly similar to Krishna’s — a man who even shares with him a name.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SAuo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F963d91e6-f476-4c65-bed8-cbbffc7c4ae2_467x702.png)

[Guru Gobind Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guru_Gobind_Singh). The Tenth Nanak. The Dashmesh Pita.

He is most famous for the creation of the [Khalsa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khalsa) in 1699 — one of the most revolutionary acts in the religious history of the world. In a single stroke he transformed an entire population that had been fragmented, demoralized, and socially stratified, into an egalitarian community of saint-soldiers bound by a shared covenant of courage and service.

But the Khalsa was not created in peace. Gobind’s life, like Krishna’s, was marked by repeated loss, warfare, betrayal, exile, and a tragic end.

He was just nine years old when his father, [Guru Tegh Bahadur](https://www.sikhiwiki.org/index.php/Martyrdom_of_Guru_Tegh_Bahadur), was executed by Aurangzeb for defending the principle of religious freedom and resisting oppression. His severed head was brought back to Anandpur Sahib and placed before the young Gobind.

He fought twenty one wars during a short life of only forty one years — never for conquest, never for revenge, never for personal ambition, but always as a last resort, always in defence of the principle that human beings should not be compelled by violence to abandon what they hold sacred.

During the [second siege of Anandpur Sahib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_siege_of_Anandpur), the Guru and his Sikhs had been trapped inside the city for months. Supplies were exhausted and the people were running hungry. [Forty of his Sikhs](https://www.sikhiwiki.org/index.php/Chali_Mukte), broken by deprivation, deserted him. The Mughals and the Hill Rajas, having promised safe passage, broke their word the moment the Guru’s forces emerged from the gates and attacked. In the following chaos and the flooding of the Sirsa river, Gobind was separated from his mother and his two younger sons, never to see them again.

He escaped with his two elder sons and a small band of Sikhs to the village of [Chamkaur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Chamkaur). There they were followed by a Mughal army that numbered in the tens of thousands. They themselves were forty.

His elder son [Ajit Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Chamkaur), eighteen years old, asked for permission to fight. The Guru embraced him and sent him out, knowing he would be martyred. Then his second son [Jujhar Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jujhar_Singh), fourteen years old, asked for permission to fight. The Guru embraced and sent him out too, knowing that his fate was the same as his elder brother. Both were killed that day, right in front of their father’s eyes.

His two younger sons — [Zorawar Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zorawar_Singh_\(Sikhism\)) and [Fateh Singh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fateh_Singh_\(Sikhism\)) — had been separated during the flight and captured along with their grandmother, Mata Gujri. They were presented with the same ultimatum as their grandfather: convert to Islam or be killed. Zorawar and Fateh — only eight and five years old respectively — refused. They were bricked alive into a wall. Mata Gujri died on receiving the news.

When Gobind Singh learned of the fate of his younger sons, he took an arrow, plucked a handful of grass from the ground, and said: _“What use is it to put out a few sparks when you have kindled a mighty flame instead?”_ The sparks were his four sons. The flame was the Khalsa.

After escaping Chamkaur, he was almost entirely alone — no army, no resources, no food, no shelter — moving through the Machhiwara jungle in the dead of winter with the Mughal forces hunting him. He had been wandering for days, sleeping on the bare ground, his feet blistered, his clothes torn by thorns.

And it was here, in this jungle, in these conditions, having just lost everything, that he wrote the shabd [_Mittar Pyare Nu_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mittar_Pyare_nu):

> ਮਿੱਤਰ ਪਿਆਰੇ ਨੂੰ ਹਾਲ ਮੁਰੀਦਾ ਦਾ ਕਹਿਣਾ ॥  
> ਤੁਧੁ ਬਿਨੁ ਰੋਗੁ ਰਜਾਈਆ ਦਾ ਓਢਣ ਨਾਗ ਨਿਵਾਸਾ ਦੇ ਹਹਿਣਾ ॥  
> ਸੂਲ ਸੁਰਾਹੀ ਖੰਜਰ ਪਿਆਲਾ ਬਿੰਗ ਕਸਾਈਆਂ ਦਾ ਸਹਿਣਾ ॥  
> ਯਾਰੜੇ ਦਾ ਸਾਨੂੰ ਸੱਥਰੁ ਚੰਗਾ ਭਠ ਖੇੜਿਆ ਦਾ ਰਹਿਣਾ ॥
> 
> Tell the beloved friend about the state of his devotees.  
> Without you, we feel sick wrapping ourselves in our quilts; it’s like living in a snakepit.  
> The flask is a thorn in the side, the cup a dagger — it’s like enduring the butcher’s blows.  
> Better to sleep on the ground near the beloved; staying in the village is like being in a furnace.

Four sons gone, his mother gone, his city gone, his community scattered, alone in a forest in winter — and what he asked for, what he _longed_ for, was not the return of his sons, nor food or shelter, nor deliverance from his enemies, but only the company of the Divine. Everything else — even the tragic events he had just faced — was simply what had happened.

This is not poetic exaggeration, but the consequence of a psychology whose identity is anchored at a depth that loss cannot reach.

## 7. The Indestructible Centre

Why did catastrophic loss not shatter Guru Gobind Singh and Krishna as it shattered Andrew Laeddis and Patachara?

The surface answer is that they were extraordinary, divine men. But to say this is to conveniently create an unbridgeable gap between us and them and be relieved of the responsibility of examining our own inner structure.

Both Krishna and Gobind Singh walked on this same earth with the same body as us. Both bled, wept, and felt cold. They were not exempt from the conditions of human life. What differed was not the intensity of their experience but the ground on which that experience landed.

Andrew and Patachara’s identities and worldview were built entirely around things which were temporal and certain to pass. To build the self-concept upon such material is not just unwise but psychologically suicidal.

**If there is an identity that cannot collapse, it must be anchored in something that cannot be taken.** Is there something at all which cannot be taken, which is not transitory and hence permanent? Philosophers have wrestled with this question for centuries. Some have called it God, Truth, Reality, the Self, or the Absolute, while others have deliberately remained silent, preferring only to strip away what is false rather than give a name to whatever lies beyond it, if at all. Whatever the approach, the practical psychological movement is similar: identification shifts away from what is transient, vulnerable, and dependent upon circumstances.

And this is what ultimately differentiated Krishna and Gobind Singh from Andrew and Patachara. They had not built their identities on anything the phenomenal world could take. Their identity was rooted in the Absolute — in that which is itself the ground of all existence, which does not come and therefore cannot go.

> कबीर सो धन संचिये, जो आगे को होय।  
> सीस चढ़ाये पोटली, ले जात न देख्या कोय॥
> 
> Accumulate only that wealth which endures beyond death.  
> No one has ever carried worldly riches beyond the grave.
> 
> — Kabir

## 8. On Fearlessness

The most revolutionary and practically visible consequence of this internal shift is that you cannot scare a person whose identity is not built on anything that can be taken.

Fear, at its root, is the anticipated loss of something one is attached to — ambition, money, status, the lives of loved ones, or one’s own life. It is therefore not a personality defect but a structural inevitability — where there is attachment, there will be vulnerability, and where there is vulnerability, there will be fear. There is no escape from fear as long as the foundation of identity remains what it is.

For Gobind Singh and Krishna, fear was simply not available as a response — not because they were unaware of danger, but because there was nothing in them that loss or death could reach. Their sense of self had no stake in the continuation of the body, the survival of the clan, or the preservation of the kingdom. They had already, inwardly, died to all of that. What remains after that inner death is precisely what cannot die. This is fundamentally different from the courage which acts despite fear. Such courage is admirable, but the fear remains and is being merely overridden. In Krishna and Gobind Singh, fearlessness arose from a different source altogether — there was no fear to overcome because there was nothing left that the world could threaten.

> चिड़ियों से मैं बाज लड़ाऊं, गीदड़ों को मैं शेर बनाऊं,  
> सवा लाख से एक लड़ाऊं, तभी गोबिंद सिंह नाम कहाऊं॥
> 
> I shall empower the sparrows to vanquish the falcons,  
> I shall transform the timid into fearless lions,  
> I shall make a single warrior stand against a formidable legion of thousands —  
> Only then will I rightfully bear the name Gobind Singh.
> 
> — Guru Gobind Singh

## 9. On Psychological Time

There is a second factor that differentiated these two pairs which I discussed in my first essay at length.

The mind does not live in the present. It is continuously pulled backward into memory and forward into anticipation. The past appears as a record of what happened to _me_; the future as a projection of what will happen to _me_. Between these two movements, the actual present — the only moment that ever exists — is rarely encountered directly.

This is the mechanism through which suffering sustains itself. The grief of loss does not exist _now_, in this moment, except as a thought. The dread of what may come does not exist _now_, except as a thought. When the mind is gripped by the past or the future, it is not experiencing reality — it is experiencing its own representation of reality, filtered through accumulated conditioning.

Consider what Krishna did in the hours after the massacre, after standing alone amongst the bodies of his entire clan. He walked back to Dwarka. He went to his father and asked him to send word to Arjuna to come and escort the surviving women to safety. Then he left the city and sat alone beneath a tree. There is no recorded paralysis, no collapse into the weight of what had just happened. Each action followed from the immediate present need: the survivors needed an escort, Arjuna needed to be summoned, an aged father needed to be told. The grief was real. But it was not permitted to become the lens through which the next hour was distorted. They responded to what was present, not to the accumulated weight of what had been.

> ਵਰਤਮਾਨ ਵਿਚ ਵਰਤਦਾ, ਹੋਵਣਹਾਰ ਸੋਈ ਪਰਵਾਣਾ।  
> ਰਾਜੀ ਹੋਏ ਰਜਾਈ ਵਿਚ, ਦੁਨੀਆ ਅੰਦਰ ਜਿਉ ਮਹਿਮਾਣਾ।
> 
> He always moves in the present — alert to what is — while accepting what is to come.  
> He remains at peace in the will of the Divine, moving through the world as a guest.

This is not passivity. It is the most demanding form of active engagement — to meet each moment on its own terms, without the distortion of what was or the dread of what will be.

## 10. A Necessary Clarification

I want to be precise about what is being argued here, because it can be distorted into a shallow counsel of detachment or emotional suppression.

Memory is a biological fact and you cannot erase it. The impressions of the past will arise when stimulated, and sometimes without any stimulus at all. What can change is not the memory but the _attachment_: the relevance that you attach to the thought that arises out of memory, and the unconscious demand that life preserve what we have identified with. Gobind Singh must have felt pain when his sons were killed, as must have Krishna when his entire clan was annihilated in front of him. The question is not whether we feel loss, but what _kind_ of relationship we have with it.

> मन के मते न चालिये, मन के मते अनेक।  
> जो मन पर असवार है, सो साधु कोई एक।।
> 
> Do not walk according to the mind’s opinions, for the mind’s opinions are countless.  
> The one who has mounted over the mind — such a one alone is a true saint.
> 
> — Kabir

Most people simply let the festering wounds of the past get buried under new and fresh memories, and given enough time, the wound is buried deep enough under more immediate impressions such that it produces only a numb ache instead of the original burning and paralyzing pain. This is the general formula of “time heals”. But this is a poor remedy — firstly because the wound is not healed, only covered, and life can always dig it back up. Secondly, this does nothing to prevent new wounds, because the underlying disease — the misplaced centre of identity — has not been touched.

## 11. The Hope

Even Patachara healed at last.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aKia!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F969830ac-829b-4759-93c0-eeb3cff132a2_1402x1122.png)


After wandering naked through streets, abused and cast out, she met the Buddha, and under his guidance an inner shift began inside her. She later became a bhikkhuni — a nun of the Buddha’s Sangha — and eventually a teacher herself, helping and guiding others.

There is hope for everyone. Perhaps even Andrew Laeddis could have been saved if he had had the company of a Buddha, a Krishna, or a Gobind Singh.

This is the purpose of great lives. Not to be worshipped from a safe distance but to be evidence that even in the most adverse conditions imaginable — four sons martyred, a dynasty destroyed, an enemy empire pressing from all sides — a human being with the same body, the same nervous system, the same capacity for grief as any of us, can remain composed.

Not because they felt any less. But because what they were was not defined by what they could lose.

> ਜਉ ਤਨੁ ਤੈ ਅਪਨੋ ਕਰਿ ਮਾਨਿਓ ਅਰੁ ਸੁੰਦਰ ਗ੍ਰਿਹ ਨਾਰੀ ॥  
> ਇਨ ਮਹਿ ਕਛੁ ਤੇਰੋ ਰੇ ਨਾਹਿਨ ਦੇਖਹੁ ਸੋਚਿ ਬਿਚਾਰੀ ॥੧॥  
> ਰਤਨ ਜਨਮੁ ਅਪਨੋ ਤੈ ਹਾਰਿਓ ਗੋਬਿੰਦ ਗਤਿ ਨਹੀ ਜਾਨੀ ॥  
> ਨਿਮਖ ਨ ਲੀਨ ਭਇਓ ਚਰਨਨ ਸਿਉ ਬਿਰਥਾ ਅਉਧ ਬਿਹਾਨੀ ॥੨॥
>

> You have gambled away this jewel-like human life; you have not realized the state of the Divine.  
> You have not remained absorbed in the Divine Presence for even a single moment; your life is passing away in vain. ॥2॥
> 
> — Guru Tegh Bahadur
